Let's start from the beginning.

Setting up a new aquascape can be difficult. That's why we are here to help you.

Nano

        Pros: less space taken up in homes, easier and cheaper to scape        Cons: more frequent water changes, expensive compared to normal aquariums    

Basic

        Pros: larger fish selection, less water changes, less maintenance         Cons: larger space taken up, equipment and decor can get expensive

Although there are many dimensions an aquarium can take, there are generally two types: nano and basic. Nano aquariums are considered to be under 10 gallons (37litres) and are becoming more of a staple in aquarium keeping. They take up less space in your home, can fit practically anywhere, and look amazing when scaped with just the right amount of plants. But one thing to consider is that they are a lot more complicated to maintain. With such small space, they require more frequent water changes and can be quite expensive compared to a basic aquarium. For example, the typical 7 gallon nano aquarium (12"x12"12") can cost anywhere from $60 to $90, depending on if they complete rimless glass or black plastic lining for a bolder aesthetic. A unique but highly sought after tank is the 12-gallon long aquarium. 36" in length and 8" height can run about $110 whereas the standard 10-gallon aquarium (20"x10"x12") costs around $20 to $25.

Basic aquariums are what you would typically find at your local pet store. 10 gallon, 20 gallon tall and long, 40 gallon breeder, 75 and 125 gallon tanks. They are in most homes and have a greater variety for selecting fish due to their large space that's required to provide less stress. Because of their large size, water changes can be less frequent and filters can be cleaned less. These aquariums can serve as breeding tanks, housing for larger livestock such as discus or arowana, and can be very pleasing to look at. But there is no dismissing the fact that these basic aquariums can come in a wide range of price. Large tanks in the triple digit gallon size can run you quite a tab, but it's no where near as absurd as paying $120 for a 12-gallon tank that is not even 10" tall.
